\section{Conclusion}
This work demonstrated DoPIo and VisR's ability to distinguish porcine kidneys with acute, chronic, and combined IRIs from kidneys without any injury as well as from each other. The detected differences were correlated with histological evidence of inflammation and/or fibrosis. We also found that, by developing binary classifiers, DoPIo showed improved performance over VisR in detecting injured renal parenchyma as well in detecting renal parenchyma that have both acute and chronic IRIs. Meanwhile, VisR was superior to DoPIo in specifically identifying chronically injured kidneys. 

Considering IRI pigs as models of inflammation and fibrosis as seen in AMR, this suggests that DoPIo could act as a noninvasive surveillance tool for potential renal allograft rejections as well as nonspecific parenchymal injury. On the other hand, VisR may be preferred for specifically identifying inflammation or fibrosis. Thus, we demonstrate that DoPIo and VisR are both relevant for detecting renal pathologies in a non-invasive manner.

In the future, we aim to refine DoPIo's application to TI materials and evaluate impacts of viscosity. We also aim to extend this work by comparing the performance of DoPIo to VisR in human renal transplant recipients for the early detection of AMR and other pathologies.
